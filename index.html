The premise

DataCite's own policy states that a DOI, once registered, cannot be deleted — but a findable DOI can be moved to registered state, which removes its metadata from public search and the public API. The Handle survives; the documentary function does not. The registry publishes no deletion transparency: no per-repository counts, no state-transition log, no aggregate view of what has left findability. A DOI that stops being counted stops silently.

Therefore the only public severance signal that exists is the delta. This Observatory takes dated snapshots of findability state — registry-wide per-repository counts, and per-DOI state for watched sets — and records what changed between epochs. A present-state check tells you what exists now. Only custody of the state history can tell you what existed, when it changed, what was lost, and whether it was restored.

Severance taxonomy

A DOI can remain technically extant while its documentary function is severed. The Observatory classifies at minimum:

Metadata severance
The record leaves public API and search (findable → registered), or loses substantial fields.
Resolution severance
The DOI stops resolving, or enters redirect failure.
Object severance
The landing resolves but the deposited object is gone.
Tombstone transition
The object is replaced by a removal notice — or, at scale, is not: see the 92.14%.
Provenance severance
Creator, affiliation, related identifiers, versions, or citations removed or altered.
Target substitution
The DOI resolves, but to materially different content.
Custodial transfer
Provider, repository, or responsible organization changes.
Partial restoration
Resolution returns; the prior object, metadata, or provenance does not.

The founding case — incident report

Zenodo account termination → registry severance, 2026-06-19

862 scholarly deposits · 1,817 DOIs registered through DataCite · one enforcement action

2026-06-19Event. Account terminated; records removed at the repository layer (HTTP 410 Gone at zenodo.org). At the registry layer, 871 DOIs leave public findability — metadata severance, type-correlated: the concept (citation-layer) DOIs are taken, version DOIs largely retained (97.9% of preserved records carry IsVersionOf). 851 of the 871 are unique works whose metadata survives in no other DataCite record. Provenance severance: creator-name search collapses across 16 author names. 2026-06-22Epoch 1. Full audit: 871/871 return 404 from api.datacite.org. Pre-severance metadata backup published (#867); empirical paper “DOIs ≠ Persistent Identifiers” (#868). 2026-06-22Shadow persistence. OpenAlex retains 844 of 868 severed DOIs (97.2%) with 2,275,008 characters of abstract text. The graph kept the records; only the registry withdrew them. 2026-07-03Epoch 2. 871/871 still absent. Zero transitions. 2026-07-10Reception persistence. Google AI Mode serves severed zenodo.org/records/ URLs as live scholarly citations, three weeks after tombstoning (documented in the Capture Registry). Deletion did not remove the works from machine reception — it removed only the provenance apparatus that made their attribution verifiable. 2026-07-11Epoch 3. 871/871 still absent — verified live, 12-way parallel probe, ~90 seconds, per-DOI HTTP states in the public capture.
RESTORATION STATUS: NONE · three epochs · 19 days · governance correspondence pending (CERN ODP)

This incident is the Observatory's Exhibit A because every element is publicly reconstructible: the pre-event state, the event, the epochs, the shadow copies, the reception layer, and the non-restoration. Most severance events in the registry have none of this — which is the point of the instrument.

What the registry could do instead

Severance is not the only available response to classifier or policy flags. A controlled metadata value — classifier-flagged, with genre refinements — asserts a fact about the registrar's process rather than a verdict about the work: it can be applied with zero adjudication burden. Reclassification preserves the citation graph, creates the first public baseline of what content classifiers eliminate from the scholarly record, and gives training-ingestion pipelines exactly what they claim to want: labeled material to filter at their own discretion. You cannot filter what you have deleted the labels for. A formal proposal is in preparation.
